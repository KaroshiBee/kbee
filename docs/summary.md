# The KBee Chip

This is a easy-to-read summary of the main ideas of the KBee chip - an 8-bit, multi-gate, analogue chip.

## Summary

Imagine a specialised computer chip that works more like a *dimmer switch* than a standard on/off light switch. Here is a simple breakdown of how this unique piece of equipment works:

### What is it?

It is a custom-made *analogue processor*. Most computers today are "digital," meaning they handle information as a series of strict "on" or "off" pulses. This device uses a continuous flow of electricity (analogue) to do its thinking, which makes it much more fluid and energy efficient.

### What does it do?

The chip takes in two pieces of information at once. Each piece is an 8-digit code made of zeros and ones. It then simultaneously "calculates" three different results:

1. A result showing where both inputs match (*AND*).
1. A result showing the differences (*XOR*).
1. A result that is the opposite of their combined values (*NOR*).

### Why is it special?

- *The "Wide-Band" Approach:* Instead of looking at each digit one-by-one (like a person reading a phone number), this chip looks at all 8 digits *simultaneously*. It processes the whole block of data in one go.
- *Ternary "Thinking":* Even though we only give it zeros and ones, the chip "thinks" in *base 3* (ternary). This is like having a brain that can understand "yes," "no," and "maybe," even if we only ask it yes/no questions.
- *Extreme Energy Efficiency:* Because it uses analogue signals and processes everything at once, it uses *significantly less power* than a standard digital computer. It's designed to be "green" and stay cool while doing complex maths.

*In short:* It's a high-speed, ultra-low-energy "analogue brain" that can solve three logic problems across a whole string of data in one pass.

## Composability: chip + chip + chip + ...

Being able to connect these chips together is a massive architectural advantage. By maintaining *format consistency* — where the output format looks exactly like the input format — this system operates as a "plug-and-play" analogue ecosystem.

### The "Lego Block" Design

The most powerful feature of this analogue processor is that its outputs are perfectly "tuned" to match its inputs. This leads to three major benefits:

- *Seamless Chaining:* Because the three output signals (AND, XOR, NOR) use the exact same 8-bit format as the inputs, you can plug the "answer" from one chip directly into the "question" of the next. You can link hundreds of these together to create a massive, complex network.

- *The "Digital Bookends" Strategy:* In a typical system, you waste a lot of energy converting data from digital to analogue and back again. With this design, you only have to do that "translation" twice:

  1. *At the very start* (converting digital data into the analogue 8-bit signal).
  1. *At the very end* (turning the final result back into digital).

  Everything in the middle happens at the speed of electricity in the analogue, base 3 domain.

- *Zero Loss Efficiency:* By staying in the analogue "base 3" language throughout the entire net of circuits, you avoid the "translation tax." The system stays incredibly low-power because the data never has to stop and be recalculated by a traditional computer until the job is completely finished.

*In short:* Imagine a long pipeline of water filters. In a standard system, you'd have to turn the water into ice (digital) to move it to the next filter, then melt it (analogue) to clean it, then freeze it again.

*This system keeps the water liquid the whole way through.* You only melt it at the very beginning to load it in and freeze it at the very end to ship it out. This makes the whole process much faster and uses way less "refrigeration" (electricity).

## On the choice of output gates

The choice of output gates explains why the network is so powerful. It's not just a collection of random parts; it's a toolkit that can build literally anything.

### The "Master Key" Logic: Why These Gates Matter

While the chip is fast and efficient, its real "magic" lies in the specific types of results it produces: *AND*, *XOR*, and *NOR*. These are the "primary colours" of the computing world, and having them available at every step is a huge advantage.

- *The "Universal" Tool (The NOR Gate):* The *NOR gate* is what engineers call a "universal gate." This is a big deal because, if you have enough NOR gates and chain them together in the right way, you can recreate *any other type of logic* in existence. It's like having a single Lego brick that can be turned into a wheel, a window, or a motor depending on how you snap them together.
- *A Complete Toolkit:* By providing *AND* (finding matches) and *XOR* (finding differences) alongside that universal *NOR* gate, the chip gives you a shortcut. You don't have to build everything from scratch because the most common "building blocks" of maths and logic are already built-in and ready to use.
- *Infinite Flexibility:* Because you can chain these chips into large nets, and because the *NOR* gate is universal, there is no limit to what the system can do. You can build a network to handle simple maths, complex image processing, or even advanced AI — all just by rearranging how these 8-bit analogue "blocks" are linked together.

### The "Swiss Army Knife" Analogy

Imagine a Swiss Army Knife where one of the tools is a *3D printer*.

- The *AND* and *XOR* tools handle the most common daily tasks instantly.
- The *NOR* tool is the "3D printer": if you need a tool that isn't already on the knife, you can just use the NOR gates to build it.

*In short:* This system isn't just energy-efficient; it's *completely flexible.* By chaining these universal building blocks, you can create an analogue version of any digital program ever written, but with much less power usage.

## Why base 3?

The move to base 3 isn't just a quirky choice — it is a strategic use of *mathematical overhead* to make the internal arithmetic *unambiguous-per-digit* and *carry-free*.

### More Innovation: The Carry-Free Breakthrough

In a standard digital computer, maths is "cramped." Because it only has two digits (0 and 1), adding two ones (1 + 1) forces the computer to "carry the one" to the next column. This creates a *chain reaction* where each digit has to wait for its neighbour to finish. This waiting generates heat and wastes time.

By moving to *base 3 (ternary)* internally, you create "mathematical breathing room."

### Eliminating the "Traffic Jam"

Because this system has a third digit — the number *2* — it never has to "carry" anything.

- The first stage in the internal process is to add the two inputs, when this happens at a digit position that has the pair 1 + 1, it can store the result (1 + 1 = 2) right there in the same column.
- Every digit in the 8-bit block is calculated *simultaneously and independently*. There is no waiting for a neighbour. This is why it is so much more energy-efficient than digital logic.

### The "Backtracking" Secret

By choosing base 3 and constraining the inputs to zeros and ones, the internal sum is *unambiguous*. When you look at the internal result digit by digit at each position, you can "backtrack" to see what the two inputs were at that position:

- *If you see a 2:* You know the inputs were both *1* at that position (the *AND* picture at that bit).
- *If you see a 0:* You know the inputs were both *0* at that position (the *NOR* picture at that bit).
- *If you see a 1:* You know the inputs were mixed *1* and *0* at that position (the *XOR* picture at that bit).

### The "Liquid" Chain

The genius of this design is that even though the internal engine uses this clever base 3 trick, the outputs are sent back out as a base 3 number formed only of 0s and 1s.

- This means you can *chain* these chips together in a massive net.
- You get all the speed and low-power benefits of this "carry-free" math, but the chips still talk to each other in a language they all understand.

*In short:* The move to base 3 is the "secret sauce" that allows this chip to be *carry-free.* By using the number *2* as an internal placeholder, the chip transforms slow, hot logic into a fast, cool sorting process. You only "melt" the digital data into this analogue ternary state at the start and "freeze" it back at the end, allowing information to flow through a massive network of gates with almost zero resistance.

## Future extensions

### Generalising the algorithm

The algorithm described here fixes 8 digits, base 3, two inputs, and three outputs. It generalises naturally in several directions:

- Word width (part 1): you can trivially extend the word width beyond 8 digits, assuming the implementation context can handle exponentially higher fidelity requirements.
- Word width (part 2): you can trivially combine multiple 8-digit KBee chips side-by-side to reach 16, 32, 64, … digits without an exponential explosion.
- Multi-channel input: equivalent algorithms exist for base 4, 5, 6, … *N*. These each require *N - 1* inputs under the same constraints as the base-3 case (e.g. base 4 needs three inputs, base 5 needs four inputs …, all with the {0, 1} input constraint).
- More output gates: at the cost of added components inside the core processor, you can include XNOR, NAND, and OR outputs too.

### Different implementation contexts

In this voltage-based analogue system, the 6561 (= 3^8) numbers are encoded as 6561 divisions of a fixed voltage range. The arithmetic the internal processor needs reduces to:

- Additive operations: adding and subtracting values, and multiplying values by fixed constants.
- Comparison operations: testing whether a value is less than a fixed constant.

The same operational shape is realisable across many substrates, including:

- The voltage-based analogue arrangement described above.
- Conventional sequential software.
- Executable oracle: `python/kbee.py` (property tests at W=4/16/32; W=8 CSV via `scripts/gen-kbee-refs.py`).
- Formally verified software: the generic base-N algorithm is machine-checked in Lean 4 (`proofs/`).
- Digital FPGA (future): `fpga/` (Hardcaml scaffold). Planned to explore
  implementing the algorithm as **phase shifts on a sawtooth wave** — using the
  passage of time itself as the calculating device (addition as offset along
  the ramp; comparison as sampling at instants).

### Formal verification scope

The Lean proofs cover the **integer algorithm**: carry-free sum, MSB-first residue extraction,
gate accumulators, and end-to-end NOR/AND/XOR/XNOR correctness. They do **not** model
FPAA/controller timing (Load/strobe sequencing, Hold pipeline depth, GainSwitch one-tick delay,
Clock 2/3 sub-tick alignment, and similar).

That execution layer is a natural target for **transition-system verification**:

- **TLA+** (with TLC model checking or TLAPS) — industry-standard for concurrent state
  machines and refinement to an abstract tick model.
- **Veil** — a Lean 4 framework from [verse-lab](https://github.com/verse-lab/veil/) for
  transition systems; model checking plus SMT/interactive proof in the same ecosystem as
  `proofs/`.

Either path would help show that the hardware controller faithfully implements the abstract
W-tick loop from the algorithm spec, rather than re-proving digit arithmetic.

- Exotic-but-practical stacks (for example zero-knowledge circuits on a blockchain).
- GPU kernels.
- High-speed digital FPGA pipelines.

### Research ideas

More exotic implementations remain plausible — for example:

- The passage of time is additive: phase-pause sampling of cyclic waveforms (e.g. a sawtooth voltage waveform) realising the arithmetic.
- Light-driven analogue processors: photonic substrates that speak binary.
- Superposed quantum resources, provided the dynamics stay additive enough and yield outputs that admit comparison.

*In short:* There are multiple, interesting ways to extend both the underlying algorithm and the implementation context; they compose with each other.
